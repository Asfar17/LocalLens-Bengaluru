# Etiquette Context: Bangalore Social Norms

## Overview

Bangalore blends traditional South Indian values with modern cosmopolitan culture. Understanding local etiquette helps you navigate social situations smoothly and build ***genuine connections***.

## General Social Etiquette

### Greetings

| Situation | Appropriate Greeting | Notes |
|-----------|---------------------|-------|
| Formal/Professional | Handshake, **"Hello"** | Firm but not crushing |
| Traditional/Elders | **Namaste** (palms together) | Shows respect |
| Casual/Peers | **"Hi"**, **"Hey"**, wave | Relaxed |
| Kannada speakers | **"Namaskara"** | Appreciated effort |

### Addressing People

**Do**:
- Use **"Sir/Madam"** for strangers and in professional settings
- **"Anna"** (elder brother) / **"Akka"** (elder sister) for slightly older strangers
- First names only after invitation or with peers
- Add **"-ji"** suffix for respect (Sharma-ji, Ravi-ji)

**Don't**:
- Use first names with elders without permission
- Call service staff rudely - **"Anna/Akka"** works well
- Assume everyone speaks Hindi - try English first

### Personal Space

- Closer than Western norms, especially in crowds
- Same-gender physical contact (hand on shoulder) is normal
- Opposite-gender contact is conservative in public
- Queues: Expect close standing, protect your spot


## Workplace Etiquette

### Office Culture

**Timings**:
- IT companies: Flexible, 10 AM - 7 PM typical
- Traditional offices: 9 AM - 6 PM
- Startups: Highly flexible, results-oriented

**Communication**:
- Email for formal communication
- WhatsApp/Slack for quick messages
- "Let's sync up" = Let's have a meeting
- "I'll revert" = I'll respond (Indian English)

**Meetings**:
- Start 5-10 mins late is normal (IST = Indian Stretchable Time)
- Tea/coffee usually offered
- Small talk before business is expected
- Hierarchy respected - seniors speak first

**Dress Code**:
- IT/Startups: Smart casual, jeans acceptable
- Corporate: Business formal
- Fridays: Often casual
- Avoid shorts in office

### Hierarchy & Respect

- Address seniors formally until told otherwise
- Don't contradict seniors publicly
- "Yes" sometimes means "I heard you" not "I agree"
- Indirect communication is common

## Dining Etiquette

### Restaurant Behavior

**Ordering**:
- Sharing dishes is common and encouraged
- "By-two" (sharing one item) is perfectly acceptable
- Vegetarian and non-veg often ordered separately
- Specify spice level upfront

**Eating**:
- Eating with right hand is traditional (left considered unclean)
- Cutlery available everywhere, no judgment for using it
- Finishing your plate shows appreciation
- Leaving a little shows you're satisfied (context-dependent)

**Paying**:
- Fighting for the bill is a ritual - offer genuinely
- Splitting is acceptable among friends
- Elders/hosts usually pay
- Tipping: 5-10% in restaurants


### Home Visits

**Do**:
- Remove shoes at the door (look for shoe rack)
- Bring a small gift (sweets, fruits, flowers)
- Accept offered food/drink - refusing is impolite
- Compliment the home/food
- Wash hands before eating

**Don't**:
- Arrive exactly on time (5-15 mins late is fine)
- Enter kitchen without invitation
- Refuse food repeatedly
- Leave immediately after eating

## Public Behavior

### General Conduct

**Do**:
- Queue patiently (even if others don't)
- Offer seat to elderly, pregnant women, disabled
- Keep voice moderate in public
- Dispose trash properly (though many don't)
- Respect religious sites

**Don't**:
- Public displays of affection (hand-holding okay, kissing not)
- Loud arguments in public
- Point with feet (considered disrespectful)
- Touch people's heads (sacred in Indian culture)
- Photograph people without permission

### Temple Etiquette

**Must Do**:
- Remove footwear (leave at designated area)
- Dress modestly (no shorts, cover shoulders)
- Maintain silence inside
- Follow clockwise direction (pradakshina)
- Accept prasad (blessed food) with right hand

**Avoid**:
- Leather items inside some temples
- Pointing feet towards deity
- Turning back to deity
- Photography (unless permitted)

### Public Transport

**Metro**:
- Stand right, walk left on escalators
- Let passengers exit before boarding
- Women's compartment at front - respect it
- No eating inside

**Bus**:
- Board from rear, exit from front
- Offer seats to those in need
- Keep belongings secure
- Have exact change ready

**Auto**:
- Negotiate before sitting
- Don't slam the door
- Small talk is optional but appreciated


## Communication Style

### Indirect Communication

Indians often communicate indirectly to maintain harmony:

| They Say | They Might Mean |
|----------|-----------------|
| "I'll try" | Probably no |
| "Let's see" | Unlikely |
| "It's difficult" | No |
| "We'll discuss" | Not now |
| "Interesting idea" | I disagree |

### Head Wobble

The famous Indian head wobble:
- Side-to-side: Yes/Okay/I understand
- Context-dependent - watch for other cues
- Not a "no" - that's a clear head shake

### Saying No

- Direct "no" can seem rude
- Soften with "Actually...", "The thing is..."
- "Swalpa difficult" = No
- Offer alternatives when declining

## Gender Etiquette

### General

- Conservative compared to Western norms
- Public affection between couples is frowned upon
- Same-gender friendships are very close (hand-holding normal)
- Women-only spaces exist (metro compartment, queues)

### For Women

- Eve-teasing (harassment) exists - stay alert
- Dress modestly in traditional areas
- Women-only compartments/queues are your right
- Trusted apps: Ola, Uber (share ride details)

### For Men

- Don't stare - it's uncomfortable and rude
- Maintain respectful distance
- Offer help appropriately, accept "no"
- In crowded transport, be mindful of space

## Festival & Religious Etiquette

### Major Festivals

**Ugadi (Kannada New Year)**:
- Greet with "Ugadi Habbada Shubhashayagalu"
- Accept neem-jaggery mix (symbolizes life's mix)

**Ganesh Chaturthi**:
- Respect processions, they have right of way
- Don't photograph without permission

**Diwali**:
- Greet with "Happy Diwali" or "Shubh Deepavali"
- Exchange sweets with neighbors
- Expect firecrackers (earplugs help)

**Sankranti**:
- Kite flying season
- Accept "Ellu Bella" (sesame-jaggery mix)


### Religious Sensitivity

- Respect all religions - Bangalore is diverse
- Don't criticize religious practices
- Remove footwear at religious places
- Dress appropriately for religious visits

## Business Etiquette

### Meetings

- Exchange business cards with both hands
- Small talk first (family, cricket, weather)
- Tea/coffee will be offered - accept graciously
- Decisions may take time - patience required

### Negotiations

- Relationship-building comes first
- Aggressive tactics backfire
- "Final price" is rarely final
- Written agreements important

### Gifts

- Not expected but appreciated
- Avoid leather (religious sensitivity)
- Sweets are safe choice
- Don't open gifts immediately (unless asked)

## Do's and Don'ts Summary

### Do's ✓
- Learn basic Kannada phrases
- Respect elders and hierarchy
- Accept hospitality graciously
- Be patient with "Indian time"
- Dress modestly in traditional areas
- Use right hand for eating/giving
- Remove shoes when required
- Bargain at markets (not malls)

### Don'ts ✗
- Public displays of affection
- Pointing with feet
- Touching heads
- Refusing food repeatedly
- Being overly punctual (can seem eager)
- Criticizing India/culture publicly
- Losing temper in public
- Assuming everyone speaks Hindi

## Apartment & Housing Etiquette

### Moving In
- Introduce yourself to neighbors (bring sweets)
- Register with apartment association
- Understand maintenance rules and charges
- Parking allocation - don't take others' spots

### Living in Apartments
- Noise restrictions usually 10 PM - 7 AM
- Inform security about guests/deliveries
- Garbage segregation mandatory (wet/dry)
- Common area usage rules vary by complex

### Landlord Relations
- Pay rent on time (usually by 5th of month)
- Inform about maintenance issues promptly
- Get written permission for modifications
- 1-2 months notice before vacating

## Digital Etiquette

### WhatsApp Groups
- Don't spam with forwards
- Good morning messages are common (tolerate them)
- Mute groups liberally
- Ask before adding to groups

### Professional Communication
- Email for formal matters
- WhatsApp acceptable for quick work messages
- LinkedIn for professional networking
- Don't call without texting first (unless urgent)

### Online Payments
- UPI (Google Pay, PhonePe, Paytm) is king
- Always verify UPI ID before large transfers
- Screenshot payment confirmations
- "Paytm karo" = pay digitally

## Gym & Fitness Etiquette

- Wipe equipment after use
- Re-rack weights
- Don't hog machines during peak hours
- Appropriate workout attire
- Headphones for music

## Pet Etiquette

### Dog Owners
- Leash in public areas
- Clean up after your pet
- Some apartments have pet restrictions
- Dog parks: Cubbon Park, BBMP parks

### General
- Ask before petting others' dogs
- Be mindful of those afraid of animals
- Strays are common - don't feed in residential areas (controversial)

## Photography Etiquette

### Public Places
- Ask permission before photographing people
- No photography in some temples/museums
- Respect "No Photography" signs
- Be discreet in markets

### Social Media
- Ask before posting others' photos
- Tag locations thoughtfully
- Respect privacy preferences

## Queue Culture

### Where Queues Work
- Metro stations
- Movie theaters
- Banks
- Government offices (mostly)

### Where They Don't
- Bus stops (survival of the fittest)
- Auto stands
- Street food stalls
- Festival venues

### Queue Tips
- Stand close to person ahead
- Protect your spot
- Separate queues for women/seniors often exist
- "Line mein aao" = get in line

## Noise & Disturbance

### Acceptable
- Construction: 7 AM - 8 PM
- Festivals: Extended hours during major festivals
- Weddings: Can go late, especially in wedding halls

### Not Acceptable
- Loud music after 10 PM in residential areas
- Honking in hospital/school zones
- Loudspeakers without permission

### What to Do
- Talk to neighbors first
- Apartment association for building issues
- Police for persistent violations (100)

## Eco-Friendly Etiquette

### Waste Management
- Segregate: Green (wet), Blue (dry), Red (hazardous)
- E-waste: Special collection drives
- Composting encouraged in many apartments

### Water Conservation
- Bangalore faces water scarcity
- Don't waste water
- Rainwater harvesting mandatory for large buildings
- Report water wastage

### Plastic
- Carry cloth bags (plastic bags banned)
- Refuse unnecessary packaging
- Many cafes offer discounts for bringing own cups

## Cultural Sensitivity

### Religious Diversity
- Respect all religions equally
- Don't assume dietary preferences
- Be aware of religious holidays
- Avoid religious debates

### Regional Sensitivity
- Don't mock accents or languages
- Appreciate Kannada culture
- Avoid North vs South comparisons
- "Namma Bengaluru" pride is real

### LGBTQ+ Awareness
- Growing acceptance, especially in urban areas
- Pride events held annually
- Some LGBTQ+ friendly spaces exist
- Still conservative in traditional areas

## Conflict Resolution

### Approach
- Stay calm, don't raise voice
- "Swalpa adjust maadi" works wonders
- Involve neutral third party if needed
- Written complaints for serious issues

### When to Escalate
- Apartment association for building disputes
- Consumer forum for service issues
- Police for harassment/threats
- RTO for traffic disputes

## Seasonal Etiquette

### Monsoon
- Carry umbrella, don't shake it on others
- Wet footwear outside shops
- Be patient with delays
- Help others in waterlogged areas

### Summer
- Offer water to delivery persons
- Don't schedule outdoor events 12-4 PM
- AC etiquette in shared spaces

### Festival Season
- Participate in community celebrations
- Respect those who don't celebrate
- Be mindful of noise levels
- Share sweets with neighbors

## Expat & Newcomer Tips

### First Week
1. Get a local SIM card
2. Download essential apps (Ola, Uber, Swiggy, Google Pay)
3. Open bank account (Kotak, HDFC easy for newcomers)
4. Register at local police station (if required)
5. Learn 5 basic Kannada phrases

### First Month
1. Explore your neighborhood
2. Find your regular spots (grocery, pharmacy, restaurant)
3. Join local community groups
4. Understand garbage collection schedule
5. Set up broadband internet

### Integration Tips
- Attend local events and festivals
- Try local food regularly
- Learn about Karnataka's history and culture
- Make friends across communities
- Be patient with the "adjustment" culture
